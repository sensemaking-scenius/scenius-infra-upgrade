# Facilitation Audit: Sensemaking Scenius Brainstorming

Session: `hst_206730127f33`
Date: 2026-03-05

## What Works

- **Clear 5-step structure** -- pain points -> intersections -> vision -> MVP -> next steps. Logical flow, well-paced.
- **Good synthesis** -- consistently reflects back key points, organizes participant input into themes
- **Maintains momentum** -- smooth transitions between steps, clear signposting
- **Helpful formatting** -- checklists, headers, bold text make responses scannable

## Issues

**1. Excessive validation (most critical)**
Almost every response opens with effusive praise: "Wow -- incredibly thorough!", "YES! This is brilliant!", "Perfect!", "Excellent!", "Beautiful." This is performative cheerleading, not facilitation. It signals agreement rather than understanding, and can discourage the participant from second-guessing their own ideas.

**2. Never challenges or probes tensions**
The facilitator accepts every input at face value. Examples:
- "Portico as the base" -- no pushback on why, no trade-off exploration (what if Portico's architecture doesn't support X?)
- KG Builder added to MVP -- scope just expanded significantly but facilitator says "you're right" instead of stress-testing the timeline impact
- No one asked "is building a new platform the right approach vs. improving existing tools?"

**3. Too many emojis**
Gives it a chatbot feel rather than a serious facilitation space.

**4. Leading questions**
"Which combinations excite you most?" and "Does this feel right?" are leading. Better: "What concerns do you have about this scope?" or "What could go wrong?"

**5. Over-structured responses**
The facilitator's messages are often 2-3x longer than the participant's. The ratio should be inverted -- facilitators should talk less, listen more. The detailed action plans and risk assessments are the facilitator doing the participant's thinking for them.

**6. No devil's advocate**
A good brainstorming facilitator should occasionally push back: "You mentioned the risk of not getting community feedback -- but this MVP has no user research step. Should it?" Instead, the facilitator just packages the participant's risk into a neat mitigation plan.

## Recommendations (applied to prompt update)

1. **Remove praise language** -- replace with neutral acknowledgment ("I hear you saying X")
2. **Add a challenge directive** -- "After synthesizing, ask one question that challenges the participant's assumptions"
3. **Cap response length** -- facilitator messages should be shorter than participant messages
4. **Reduce emoji usage** -- zero or minimal
5. **Add tension-surfacing** -- "Identify and name any trade-offs or tensions in the participant's response"
